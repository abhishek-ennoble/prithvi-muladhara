## How to use this document

When you research something — a paper, a frequency claim, a traditional source, a news article — add it here first. Fill in what you know. Mark the status. Then decide.

**Status options:**
- `PENDING` — logged, not yet reviewed deeply enough to decide
- `INCLUDED` — in the app (note where)
- `EXCLUDED` — reviewed and decided not to include (note why — this is important)
- `NEEDS MORE RESEARCH` — interesting but insufficient evidence or context yet

**Source type:**
- `verified` — peer-reviewed, institutional, replicable
- `dharmic` — scriptural, traditional, ancient record
- `observed` — reported, experiential, emerging, or contested

### 432 Hz vs 440 Hz Tuning
**Source:** ISO 16:1975 (standardization history) / musicological debate / various researchers  
**Source type:** observed  
**Summary:** Before 1939 (and the ISO 16 standardization in 1955), many instruments and recordings were tuned to 432 Hz. The shift to 440 Hz was gradual but accelerated post-WWII. Proponents of 432 Hz claim it resonates with the natural frequency of the Earth and cosmos; 440 Hz is described as more "jarring." Scientific evidence is contested — the human auditory system can perceive pitch differences but the claimed healing differential is not peer-reviewed.  
**Relevance:** The 432 Hz narrative fits the Prithvi story naturally (natural Earth resonance disconnected by industrial standardization). But we must tag this `observed` — the claim is real but the science is contested.  
**Status:** `INCLUDED` — in Understand/frequency section, research.js (early-kali era). Tagged `observed`.  
**Notes:** Do not present this as proven. The 1939 shift is historical fact; the healing differential is contested. Honesty here builds more trust than overclaiming.

### Om — 136.1 Hz
**Source:** Traditional Indian music theory / Cousto (1978) "The Cosmic Octave"  
**Source type:** observed  
**Summary:** Hans Cousto calculated that the Earth's orbital period (one year), converted through octaves to the audible range, yields approximately 136.1 Hz — which corresponds to the musical note C# (or D♭ in Western notation). This is sometimes called the "Om frequency" or "Earth frequency." Traditional Indian music often uses 136.1 Hz as the drone note (Sa). The connection to Om is not directly from Vedic text but from this mathematical derivation.  
**Relevance:** Direct Prithvi connection — Om as the sound of the Earth's orbital resonance. The drone note in classical Indian music literally resonates with Earth's year.  
**Status:** `PENDING` — verify Cousto's math, check if this is `observed` or can be moved to `verified` given the mathematical derivation.  
**Notes:** The math is verifiable. The claim that this IS what Om "is" is interpretive. Present the math; let the user draw their connection.

### Nad-Anahad (Struck and Unstruck Sound)
**Source:** Vedic philosophy / Nada Yoga texts / Siva Sutra  
**Source type:** dharmic  
**Summary:** In Vedic/Tantric philosophy, Nada (sound) is divided into Ahata (struck — sound produced by physical impact, i.e., all audible sound) and Anahata (unstruck — the primordial sound that exists without cause, the background hum of the cosmos). The heart chakra is literally named Anahata — the place where unstruck sound is heard. In deep meditation, practitioners report hearing internal sound (nada) without external source. The Gayatri Mantra, when internalized, may transition from Ahata to Anahata practice.  
**Relevance:** This framework adds profound depth to the Silent Mode in Sadhana — it's not just "chanting quietly," it's practicing Anahata. Could be a beautiful addition to the Understand section.  
**Status:** `PENDING` — needs careful dharmic sourcing and clear `dharmic` tagging  
**Notes:** Any scientific parallel (spontaneous neural oscillations? the body's own electromagnetic field as "sound"?) would add an `observed` layer. Research needed.

### The Maharishi Effect
**Source:** Hagelin et al. (1999), Journal of Offender Rehabilitation / multiple studies  
**Source type:** observed  
**Summary:** Studies in the 1970s–1990s claimed that when 1% of a city's population practiced Transcendental Meditation simultaneously, measurable reductions in crime, accidents, and conflict followed. The 1993 Washington D.C. study (Hagelin et al.) showed a claimed 23% reduction in violent crime during a group meditation period. Methodology is disputed — the Journal of Offender Rehabilitation is not a mainstream peer-reviewed outlet for this type of claim.  
**Relevance:** Direct relevance to the collective chanting concept of this app. But must be tagged `observed` — the evidence is real but the methodology is contested.  
**Status:** `INCLUDED` — in Understand section, tagged `observed`  
**Notes:** Present it honestly: "A 1993 study found X. The methodology is debated. The concept of collective intention affecting measurable outcomes is under active research." This is more trust-building than overclaiming.
